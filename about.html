The BSc Computer Science experience briefly summarized

Here's a breakdown of what the modules I actually took were, their associated courseworks and how that related to my skill set and knowledge base.
If you ware looking for my Final Year Project, it has its own page, click here to go to it.

The inspiration for the tone of this section

I debated on creating a purely factual list, detailing each module's contents by essentially copying their syllabuses. However, I decided against this as I was strongly inspired by the following youtube video.


Year 1

In first year, you are introduced to the very basics of computer science, and a lot of the modules here are foundational to every computer scientist's education. The courseworks hold your hand through them and provide you with a robust and thoroughly tested suite of unit tests and test harnesses. This is where you will see modules covering the basics of Object Oriented Programming (OOP), basic data structures and algorithms, linear algebra and discrete math.

  • Programming 1

    This is the module everyone knows they will have to take in their first year. We covered the basics of programming for the 2% of students who haven't written a single line of code before. Followed by the basics of Object Oriented Programming theory such as inheritance and polymorphism.

  • Programming 2 (electric boogaloo!)

    We learned how to actually make proper Java programs instead of just little basic programs. Introduction to Apache Maven and build systems, introduction to the JVM and how it works etc.

    We also each made a really bad clone of Tetris under the name TetrECS, which had multiplayer functionality which was really easy to cheat (I did no such thing :3).

    You can find the code for my version of TetrECS on my github here.

  • Computer Systems 1

    This module covers the basics of computer hardware and architectures, I have recently learned that this is something not all first year students cover, which is interesting. we also got a Raspberry Pi 3B+ to play with, which we were apparently supposed to return. (If someone from the University of Southampton reads this, I did return all the hardware provided to me as is policy)

  • Foundations of Computer Science

    I don't know why my university decided to name this module as such, but it is essentially the "Linear Algebra and Discrete Mathematics" module that every first year takes. I also strongly remember everyone in my cohort massively struggling here :) The goal was simply to hold your breath through the maths, pass the module and survive.

  • Algorithmics

    Again, I don't know why this module wasn't just called "Data Structures and Algorithms" but it was basically just that. I remember quite enjoying this as it's always been a field that fascinated me. Plus, it's also going to be referred to a lot in the future when we learn about complexity and computability theory in depth.

  • Data Management

    Yet another instance of unconventional naming, this module is basically "Relational Database Management Systems" or RDBMS. We covered the basics of relational database theory, did some SQL programming and even some bash scripting basics here.

  • Software Modelling and Design

    I have no clue who decided that this should be a first year module. Formal software design and verification is a very useful thing to learn, and definitely gave me a deeper understanding of how to develop safe and verifiable software. However, this was done using the university's proprietary "Event-B" language which meant documentation was basically nonexistent. I truly believe this module would have shone a lot brighter and been much more appreciated as a second or third year optional specialization in formal systems.

  • Professional Development

    No first year at university is complete without the token "career skills" module that I'm sure comes from good intentions but is always extremely poorly executed. Having first year university students who don't even know what they want to specialize in write a CV sounds good until you realize that they would have nothing real to put into it. I think this module would have been way better conducted during second year before internship season or third year.


Year 2

The best way to summarize the second year of my degree would be "oh you thought you knew , you do not", this theme will pop up a lot when I actually break down the modules. The courseworks get bigger and more open ended, which means they give you more room for creativity and develop your complex problem solving skills but that also means that if you get lost, you get really lost.

  • Programming 3

    So you thought you knew how to program? Well actually, you only know how to write imperative procedural programs using the object oriented paradigm. Here, you can actually write programs using purely functions, goodbye classes and objects!

    Why would you want to do this? Oh of course, it's so that you can deeper understand the connection between mathematics and programming! Oh you meant when you'd actually choose to write functional code over object oriented? Uhm, when the university tells you to!

    So yeah, this module was the introduction to the abomination that is functional programming in Haskell. I am now one of the 7 people on this entire planet who can write advanced Haskell code. I don't know when I will ever need to use this skill, but I have it and it's rare so it gives me internet programmer points.

  • Software Engineering Group Project

    So you thought software engineering was just programming?? Well actually, you have to go through an entire requirements analysis and design process, communicating with the customer the whole time before you can even start writing any code! Welcome to software engineering, where the requirements are constantly changing, no one knows what the whole program is doing, but somehow we are still making progress (look at the pretty AGILE charts please!).

    Yeah, this module was just a group of 6 designing a runway redeclaration (doesn't matter what the function is really) desktop app using JavaFX. We had to use AGILE development practices, including the weekly scrum meetings, burn down charts and all of that. We designed the program, or more accurately, me and one other guy came up with the UML class diagrams. We then used a Model View Controller (MVC) design pattern to delegate tasks for front-end and back-end. Finally, we had to all use a single github repository and learn how to actually use git's version control beyond committing and pushing.

    Read more about my Software Engineering "Runway Redeclaration" app here

    Or go straight to the source code here

  • Theory of Computing

    So you thought you were out of the woods of all the theoretical computer science modules?? Time for you to learn automata theory, computability and complexity!

    In all seriousness, this module actually wasn't that bad, It is actually an incredibly beautiful part of computer science. I do believe that if I were academically inclined, I would have pursued more knowledge in theoretical computer science, but alas, I prefer having money.

  • Programming Language Concepts

    This module was a bit of a curve ball, we had heard about it in first year and in the first semester of second year that we would eventually have to design our own interpreter. Obviously, this was terrifying news to first years who had just about figured out how to use GCC and Clang to compile their first C helloworld.c program.

    This module covered how programming languages actually worked on a conceptual and theoretical level. How they are similar to human languages with syntaxes and grammars. How we lex them into tokens and parse those into expressions that we can then evaluate.

    I actually really enjoyed this branch of study, which I come to know now as "compiler theory". We eventually had to design and implement an interpreter for a Toy language using Haskell. Initially, I thought that it was incredibly stupid that we do this using a functional language like haskell, but realizing that it was between that and C, I understand why the university chose haskell. Haskell's functional nature makes it really simple to manipulate data structures, like what you'd find in a parse tree, and then evaluate the tree systematically using defined rules.

    Read more about my Toy language STQL and its interpreter I designed here

    Or go straight to the source code here

  • Computer Systems 2

    This was the only optional module that I was able to take in year 2, I chose this because I really enjoyed Computer Systems 1 in first year and always wanted to have a better understanding of hardware and low-level systems programming. This module introduced embedded systems theory and concepts, as well as some operating system concepts such as prioritization and scheduling. It also gave insight into how buses and IO work as well as how memory architectures (ex: von neumann) work.

    We were also given a Raspberry Pi Pico microcontroller and basically told to have fun and build some cool little projects with it. This isn't hyperbole, we were actually told that we just had to build stuff, document and upload it and then we'd get the mark for this part of the module. I absolutely loved this open ended teaching approach, and I fell in love with microcontrollers and electronics even more because of this module.

  • Intelligent Systems

    The name of this module should have been "Introduction to Artificial Intelligence" as it covered basically the concepts and theory that underlay modern Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning. I have never been much of an AI person, unlike some of my contemporaries so I found the mathematical background tedious rather than intriguing and I found the lack of practical aspects to this module to be a detriment to engagement.

    I think that if we were actually allowed to implement and our own algorithms or actually get hands on with some of the existing ones, this modules would have been a lot more interesting than just sitting through lectures. I remember the countless hours of just learning different searching algorithms in theory and having to "run" those algorithms manually in the exam. An extremely tedious module with a lot of wasted potential.

  • Distributed Systems and Networking

    I don't know why this module wasn't split up into 2 smaller ones, it basically was when we took it anyway. We started with the networking half which was as expected, an introduction to network architectures following the OSI 7-layer model. That was pretty unremarkable, pretty theory heavy and more or less got the points across.

    The distributed systems part of this module was more interesting, with us having to implement our own distributed networked file storage system in Java. We have to account for concurrency, consistency and reliability and overall it was really interesting dealing with real-world distributed systems problems. The most important takeaway from this module though was the ACID principles of atomicity, consistency, isolation, and durability, which I actually ended up applying into many other of my projects, some entirely unrelated to distributed systems.

  • Interaction Design

    Ah, we joked around about this module being "professional development 2" as it was the only non-technical module of the year. Though conceptually, this was essentially a Human-Computer interface theory module with an emphasis on User Interaction and Experience. A perfectly average module, does just about what it sets out to do and nothing more.


Year 3

If by some miracle, you've managed to survive the grade slump that is second year and pass. You will now be in third year, where you get to select four! optional modules, all of which are extremely advanced and specialized and build on existing knowledge and skills. You also get to do you dissertation, which is your first entirely unique piece of work you'll make in academia (in theory).

  • Advanced Computer Architecture

    This was an optional module choice that I chose because I wanted to learn more about computer architectures and hardware. It ended up being a very fitting continuation to the hardware architectures side of Computer Systems 2 from second year.

    We learned about CPU design from the lowest level half-adders building up to a full floating point unit. Then we learned about optimizations such as branch prediction and Single Instruction Multiple Data (SIMD).

    We also dabble into the GPU side of things with a quick overview of current architectures, which I particularly enjoyed as I could now understand technical whitepapers on the newest GPU architectures that I was already following.

  • Real-Time Computing and Embedded Systems

    This was an optional module choice that I chose because I enjoyed tinkering with the Raspberry Pi Pico microcontroller from Computer Systems 2 and wanted to learn more about embedded systems. The module ended up being more real-time systems with a bit of embedded systems but nonetheless I quite enjoyed it.

    We were given the opportunity to try and implement our own low-level real-time database, try our hand at real-time scheduling with "Cheddar" and even do a bit of FreeRTOS tinkering. This module really felt like an intro to a whole new world which I wish I had the chance to study further.

  • Computational Biology

    I chose this optional module because I thought it would be about biologically inspired computing, something along the lines of bio-inspired algorithms and systems. I was incredibly wrong, and when I realized, it was too late, I was doomed.

    The module turned out to be a glorified data science intro where we had to learn NumPy and Pandas and use that to analyze big data sets and come up with conclusions. It wasn't necessarily a bad module, it was just not what I expected and I did really badly on it because of my lack of interest in the actual subject matter. Though I should partially take the blame for not previewing the syllabus before selecting it as an optional.

  • Web Architecture and HyperText Technologies

    I initially was going to do a module called computer vision, though after sitting through weeks of digital signal processing lectures and realizing it wasn't the kind of computer vision I actually wanted to do, I decided to switch courses. This turned out to be much better than I expected as I actually quite enjoyed this module. It wasn't extremely difficult though it built up well on the concepts learned in the second year module "distributed systems and networking" and helped me gain a better understanding of the concepts that underlay the web and internet as we know it.

    We were given the opportunity to familiarize ourselves with Representational State Transfer concepts, client-server architecture and even play around with some practical RESTful APIs. One of the courseworks being to use curl to essentially play around with a RESTful API that the university used to store building information, document how it worked, and come up with ways to improve it.

  • Engineering Management and Law

    This was a required module for third year and covered just about what it says it would cover. There was the usual trite management stuff which I didn't leave much of an impression. Though I found myself quite fond of the law aspect of this module as we had to read about past cases and learn about the concept of precedence in law.



What I am looking for in a job

I am currently looking for my first job out of university, and it's important I make an intelligent choice as it will dictate the direction of my future career. I have a broad skill set which means that I can fit in and do just about anything, but it also means that to be good at something, I will have to put in the work to learn and develop that skill.

Therefore, my first job must be the right environment for someone to develop and grow, it should allow for experimentation with a broad variety of roles and responsibilities. Eventually leaning towards and specializing in the right place for me to provide the most value to my employer as well as specialize and increase my worth professionally.

I also believe that having a strong mentorship culture at the job will be a huge benefit as it would allow me to more quickly integrate into the work culture and start becoming more beneficial to the company.
